Jollof Rice

A beloved West African classic that's bursting with flavors

Jollof Rice is a staple in West African cuisine, particularly popular in Nigeria, Ghana, and Senegal. This one-pot dish combines rice with a flavorful tomato-based sauce, creating a rich and aromatic meal that's perfect for celebrations or everyday dining.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups long-grain parboiled rice
  • 6 medium-sized tomatoes
  • 2 red bell peppers
  • 2 scotch bonnet peppers (adjust to taste)
  • 2 medium onions
  • 1/4 cup vegetable oil
  • 2 tablespoons tomato paste
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tablespoon ginger, grated
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 teaspoon thyme
  • 1 teaspoon curry powder
  • 1 teaspoon smoked paprika
  • 2 cups chicken or vegetable stock
  • Salt to taste
  • 1 tablespoon butter (optional)

Instructions

  1. Prepare the Base

    Blend tomatoes, red bell peppers, scotch bonnet peppers, and one onion until smooth.

  2. Cook the Sauce

    Heat oil in a large pot. Dice the remaining onion and saut√© until translucent. Add minced garlic and grated ginger, cooking for 1 minute until fragrant.

  3. Develop the Flavor

    Add tomato paste and cook for 2-3 minutes. Pour in the blended mixture and cook on medium heat for 10-15 minutes until the sauce thickens and the raw tomato flavor disappears.

  4. Add Spices

    Stir in thyme, curry powder, smoked paprika, bay leaves, and salt. Cook for another 2-3 minutes.

  5. Cook the Rice

    Rinse the rice thoroughly and add it to the pot. Stir to coat with the sauce. Pour in the stock, stir, and bring to a boil.

  6. Simmer

    Reduce heat to low, cover the pot with foil and then the lid (to trap steam). Cook for 25-30 minutes until rice is tender.

  7. Finish

    Add butter (if using), gently fluff the rice with a fork, and let it sit covered for 5 minutes before serving.

Chef's Notes

For an authentic West African experience, serve this Jollof Rice with fried plantains, grilled chicken, or beef. The key to perfect Jollof is allowing the tomato base to cook down until the oil separates, which removes the raw tomato flavor and creates that distinctive taste.
